## Repository overview

This repository groups five language channels into one structured access layer:

- Chechen
- Ingush
- Avar
- Circassian
- Hungarian

Its purpose is precise: give this historically significant and underserved cluster a focused and practical place for discovery, integration, and multilingual Islamic content workflows.

---

## Why this repository exists

Four of these five languages — Chechen, Ingush, Avar, and Circassian — belong to the North Caucasus, one of the most historically resilient Islamic regions in the world. Communities here have maintained Islamic identity through centuries of imperial pressure, forced displacement, and political constraint.

Hungarian extends the repository into Central Europe, where a small but active Muslim minority community has real native-language content access needs.

What connects all five is not a single geography.

It is a shared condition: Islamic communities operating in politically complex or minority contexts where native-language Islamic digital infrastructure lags significantly behind community need.

This repository creates a cleaner layer for:

- cross-regional discoverability
- structured Islamic content access
- multilingual publishing workflows
- localization and deployment
- educational product building
- cleaner repository segmentation

---

## Why this cluster works

Chechen and Ingush are closely related Nakh languages of the eastern North Caucasus — communities with one of the most documented histories of Islamic resilience under adversity. Avar is the largest indigenous language of Dagestan, a republic with deep multi-century Islamic scholarly traditions. Circassian covers the diaspora and homeland communities of the northwestern Caucasus — a people whose forced displacement in the 19th century created Muslim minority communities across Turkey, Jordan, Syria, and beyond.

Hungarian covers Central Europe's Muslim minority — small in number but present, and deserving of the same structured Islamic content access as larger communities.
